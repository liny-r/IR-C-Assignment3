Part E(i) — Competing Risks Speaker Script
==========================================

Slide 1 — Title
"I'll be walking through Part E(i) — how we apply a competing risks framework to separate mortgage prepayment from default."

---

Slide 2 — Vintage era
"This chart shows the breakdown of prepayment, default, and active loans by origination year. Each bar sums to 100%. The 2007 cohort is the starkest case: around 8% of loans defaulted, almost nothing remains active. Post-2020 is the opposite extreme — nearly all still active, almost no prepayments. Vintage year — the origination year — dominates FICO and LTV as the primary risk driver."

---

Slide 3 — Two competing fates
"The duration histograms confirm two fundamentally different borrower stories. Prepayment peaks at around 24 months — borrowers exercise the refinancing option quickly when rates drop. Default peaks at around 60 months — it's a gradual process of financial deterioration. Two different shapes require two distinct hazard functions; a single combined model cannot represent both."

---

Slide 4 — Two estimators (KM vs AJ)
"Both estimators track exits at each point in time, but they handle the other cause differently. Kaplan-Meier removes defaulted loans from the denominator, treating them as if they simply disappeared — randomly censored. That makes the denominator artificially small, inflating every prepayment increment. Aalen-Johansen keeps all exits in the denominator and multiplies by the overall survival probability just before each event time. As loans leave for any reason, that weight shrinks, so no single cause gets over-credited. The consequence: KM probabilities can sum past 100%; AJ always partitions exactly to 1."

---

Slide 5 — AJ CIF partition
"The Aalen-Johansen CIF gives the correct probability partition. At 10 years: 83.3% prepaid, 2.2% defaulted, 14.5% still active — those three numbers sum to exactly 100%. That partition identity is impossible under Kaplan-Meier. The prepayment curve also shows burnout: the slope flattens after around month 40 as the most rate-sensitive borrowers have already exited."

---

Slide 6 — KM bias
"Here is the economic cost of using KM. Defaulted loans are systematically the riskiest borrowers — the least likely to prepay. Dropping them from the denominator inflates every prepayment increment. The bias compounds over time: 0.45 percentage points at 5 years, 1.3 at 10, 2.2 at 20. In dollar terms, a 1.3 pp overstatement at the 10-year horizon translates to roughly $1 million of mispriced duration in a $100 million MBS pool."

---

Slide 7 — Stratified CIFs
"Stratifying by borrower characteristics tells two separate stories. FICO separates default by six times — from 1% for high-FICO borrowers to 6% for sub-680 — but barely separates prepayment. A high credit score gives access to refinancing; it does not create the incentive. LTV doubles default risk but leaves prepayment curves nearly identical. Vintage dominates everything: GFC loans reach 4% default CIF while post-2013 approaches zero, and post-2020 prepayment is nearly flat due to rate lock-in."

---

Slide 8 — Cause-specific Cox model
"To move from descriptive CIFs to a regression model, we fit one Cox model per cause. The hazard formula is identical to standard Cox — the only difference is how we handle the other cause: those exits are censored at the time they leave. So in the prepayment model, every defaulted loan is treated as censored when it defaults, and vice versa. This keeps the two mechanisms cleanly separated — the coefficient vector for prepayment is estimated entirely from prepayment events, with no contamination from the default signal. We fit 14 features in total: 10 numeric including credit score, LTV, rate incentive, and the delinquency measures from our partner's data, plus four categorical dummies for loan purpose and occupancy."

---

Slide 9 — Cause-specific Cox forest plot
"Here are the results. Fitting separate Cox models for each cause makes the mechanism visible. Rate incentive is the clearest example: strong positive effect on prepayment, near zero for default — it's a pure refinancing signal. Max delinquency is the strongest separator overall: large negative for prepayment, large positive for default. The sign reversals are visible as bars pointing in opposite directions for the same covariate."

---

Slide 10 — Opposite signs table
"This table makes the sign reversals precise. The naive combined model — the middle column — blends both signals and misrepresents both. Max Delinquency is the starkest case: the naive coefficient is -0.123, hiding a spread of +0.543 between the two cause-specific estimates. Each extra month delinquent raises default hazard by 35% while cutting prepayment hazard by 22%. A combined model does not just lose precision — it gives the wrong direction for credit variables."

---

Slide 11 — Key Takeaways
"To summarize: default and prepayment are genuine competing risks — a naive Cox model conflates two economically opposite processes. KM overstates prepayment probability by up to 2.2 percentage points at 20 years, which is economically meaningful for MBS pricing. Vintage era is the dominant risk driver: the 2007 cohort defaults at 6 times the long-run average, while post-2020 prepayment is frozen by rate lock-in. FICO and rate incentive operate through separate channels — each is essentially invisible to the other cause. And Fine-Gray matches cause-specific Cox here because default is only 1.6% of loans — the distinction matters in crisis or subprime pools with much higher competing-event rates."
