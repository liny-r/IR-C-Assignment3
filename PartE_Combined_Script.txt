Part E — Speaker Script (7-minute version)
==========================================

─── E(i): Competing Risks ───────────────────────────────────────────────

[Title card]
I'll cover Part E.

[Prepaid and default are competing risks]
Loans exit in three ways: prepayment, default, or full payoff at maturity. Prepayment peaks around month 24 as borrowers refinance; default peaks around month 60 from financial deterioration. Prepayment and default are the two competing risks that need separate models.

[KM vs AJ]
Our original Kaplan-Meier estimate treats competing exits as random censoring, shrinking the denominator and inflating each causes' probability. On the other hand, Aalen-Johansen keeps all exits in the denominator, so prepayment + default + active always sums to exactly 100%.

[AJ CIF partition]
Here's the AJ partition: at 10 years, 83.3% prepaid, 2.2% defaulted, 14.5% still active — those three numbers sum to 100%, which KM cannot guarantee.

[KM bias]
The KM overstats the prepayment probabilities and this bias incrases over time: 0.45pp at 5 years, 1.3pp at 10, 2.2pp at 20. 

[FICO and LTV]
These stratified CIF plots show that FICO, LTV, and vintage year all strongly separate default risk — for instance, low-FICO borrowers default at 6x the rate of high-FICO — but leave the prepayment curves very similar. Credit quality determines who can refinance, not who wants to.

[Cause-specific Cox]
We also fit a cause-specific cox model for the two causes, introducing two additional features, max delinquency and ever 90+DQ - a binary flag for severe delinquency.

[Forest plot]
The results show that rate incentive and mortgage rate has a strong positive effect on prepayment. It's a refinancing signal. Max delinquency and the severe delinquency indicator signals default.

[Sign flip table]
A naive combined model blends prepayment and default into one coefficient, hiding the opposite effects. Take max delinquency: the naive combined coefficient is −0.12, suggesting delinquency slightly reduces the hazard. But cause-specific models show each extra month delinquent raises default hazard by 35% while cutting prepayment hazard by 22% — the signs are opposite, and the naive model reports neither correctly. Using a combined model doesn't just lose precision; it gives the wrong answer for credit risk variables.

[Key Takeaways E(i)]
Bottom line: KM overstates prepayment, vintage dominates FICO and LTV, and a combined Cox model gives the wrong sign for credit variables.

─── E(ii): Time-Varying Covariates ──────────────────────────────────────

[Title card]
Now we extend Cox to update covariates every month instead of fixing them at origination.

[Static vs Andersen-Gill]
Static Cox takes a snapshot at origination and never updates. In contrast, the Andersen-Gill extension adds one row per loan per month. We introduced four time varying covariates: rate incentive which is the live spread between the borrower's coupon and today's market rate plus origination rate, current LTV as equity builds, unemployment, and HPI. The two models share the same origination features — the only difference is whether those four variables are frozen at origination or tracked month by month.

[AG model]
This is the formula for the AG model. The hazard formula is identical to standard Cox — the only change is x(t) updates each month. One loan contributes one row per month of its life.

[Feature trajectories]
This is what the static model discards. For instance, the 2020 vintage started in-the-money but turned deeply negative after 2022 — rate lock-in. The 2007 vintage shows the opposite path. These channels are invisible at origination.

[Coefficient comparison]
Three coefficients change meaningfully when we switch to monthly updates.

Rate: the static model uses the origination rate, which tells you what era the loan was made in but nothing about today. TV Cox uses rate incentive — the live gap between the borrower's coupon and current market rates. That gap is what drives the actual decision to refinance, so it becomes the dominant predictor.

Unemployment: in the static model, originating during high unemployment predicts slower prepayment — those borrowers are riskier. In TV Cox the sign flips, because months of high current unemployment in the data happened to coincide with the Fed cutting rates aggressively. So current unemployment ends up proxying for low rates, not distress.

HPI: the static model captures home prices at origination. TV Cox tracks current home price growth. When prices are rising, borrowers build equity faster, which makes it easier to qualify for a cash-out refinance — so rising current HPI raises prepayment speed in a way the origination snapshot cannot see.

[C-index]
TV Cox beats static at every horizon up to 60 months. Beyond 70 months, static overtakes — loans surviving that long are heavily filtered rate-locked borrowers whose current covariates reflect why they haven't prepaid yet, not how likely they are to prepay next. The time-varying features encode the wrong signal for this survivor group, and origination features discriminate better.

[Key Takeaways E(ii)]
Monthly updates add useful information at short horizons. Rate incentive is the key driver. However, TV cox degrades beyond 70 months.

─── E(iii): Neural Survival Models ──────────────────────────────────────

[Title card]
Now we go beyond Cox with a Transformer that reads the full monthly history of each loan.

[Why beyond Cox]
Cox assumes covariate effects are constant over time. Two loans can have the same origination snapshot but completely opposite histories — only a sequence model can distinguish them.

[Architecture rationale]
We chose a Transformer over some other alternatives because it can directly attend to any point in a 120-month sequence — a rate shock at month 6 shaping behavior at month 60 is easy to represent.

[Architecture]
Standard architecture. 120 monthly vectors per loan, projected to 64 dimensions, two Transformer encoder layers with causal masking. Two output heads produce prepayment and default hazards. The competing-risk chain product guarantees CIF1 + CIF2 + survival = 1 at every month.

[Training dynamics]
The left panel shows NLL collapse: 98.4% of loan-months have no event, so predicting near-zero hazard for everyone already minimizes the loss — the rare 1.6% of events can't push it further. Cox avoids this because partial likelihood only ranks loans at event times, never predicts absolute hazard, so class imbalance has no trivial solution. The right panel shows AUC holds steady around 0.6 — the ranking loss keeps relative ordering correct even though absolute hazard values are unreliable. Discrimination is preserved; calibration is not.

[Gradient sensitivity]
Rate incentive and current LTV drive prepayment; FICO and unemployment drive default — replicating the E(i) cause-specific Cox findings without imposing them.

[Feature importance over lifecycle]
Rate incentive dominates early months; current LTV rises later as equity accumulates and starts gating refinancing. This time-resolved view is impossible in Cox.

[Model performance]
For default, our model matches DeepCox at every horizon with 6x fewer training loans — the full covariate history compensates for data volume. Prepayment lags DeepCox due to the NLL collapse.

[Key Takeaways E(iii)]
The Transformer successfully exploits sequence structure. Default AUC matches Part D's benchmark. The known limitation is calibration — the NLL collapse is the priority fix via focal-loss upweighting.

─── E(iv): Scenario Analysis ────────────────────────────────────────────

[Title card]
Finally, we stress-test prepayment under rate shocks and translate that into MBS cash flows.

[TV Cox rate channel]
TV Cox has a direct, linear rate channel: rate incentive is a live covariate, so any rate shock feeds straight into the hazard via one coefficient. A 300bp cut raises log-hazard by 0.123 — one number, fully auditable. Static models like DeepCox fix rate at origination, so shocking rates has no path into the model, or worse, goes in the wrong direction.

[Shock sensitivity]
Each 100bp rate cut raises prepayment hazard by 4.2%. The model has a linear rate channel — equal rate steps produce equal hazard steps — making results fully explainable from one coefficient.

[Survival curves]
The ±300bp range shifts 10-year survival by 6.6pp and median prepayment month by 10 months, monotonically ordered as expected.

[MBS cash flows]
Across ±300bp, Weighted Average Life ranges 0.58 years and price ranges $18.55. The key result is negative convexity: when rates fall, borrowers prepay at par, capping price upside — the $9.25 gain is slightly less than the $9.30 loss at ±300bp.

[Key Takeaways E(iv)]
TV Cox's linear rate channel makes stress results predictable and explainable. Negative convexity is correctly captured.
