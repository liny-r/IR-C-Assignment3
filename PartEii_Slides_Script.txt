Part E(ii) — Time-Varying Covariates Speaker Script
====================================================

Slide 1 — Title
"Next up we extend the Cox model to handle covariates that change every month, and how that impacts prepayment prediction accuracy."

---

Slide 2 — Motivation: static Cox discards the most important signal
"The core problem: static Cox fixes every covariate at origination. A 2020 borrower at 3% had a strong refi incentive then — but by 2023, with market rates at 7%, that incentive was −4 pp. The option went underwater. Static Cox never sees this.

The Andersen–Gill fix is one row per loan-month, updating the covariate vector x_i(t) each period. The feature table on the right shows what each model uses. Both share the nine origination features — FICO, LTV, DTI, UPB, orig_rate, loan purpose, occupancy. Static Cox adds origination-era unemployment and HPI snapshots. TV Cox replaces those with four monthly-updated features shown in red: rate incentive, current LTV, unemployment, and HPI — all tracked every month for the life of the loan."

---

Slide 3 — Model
"The hazard function looks identical to standard Cox — same baseline hazard, same exponential linear predictor — except x_i(t) is now current instead of fixed. The coefficient vector β is still estimated once across the whole dataset.

The partial likelihood sums over event times. At each event, the risk set R(t_i) contains every loan-month still active just before that event — so a single loan contributes one row to the risk set at each month of its life.

The two key new features are rate_incentive, which is the live spread between the origination coupon and today's market rate, and synthetic ELTV, which tracks the current loan-to-value as the borrower builds equity through amortization and home price appreciation. Both of these are impossible to measure at origination — they require monthly updates."

---

Slide 4 — Feature trajectories
"This chart shows exactly what the static model discards. On the left, rate incentive by vintage over loan age. The 2007 vintage started with a high origination rate and saw incentive turn positive in the early 2010s as rates fell — these borrowers refinanced heavily. The 2020 vintage started positive but turned sharply negative after 2022, killing the refi option entirely. The static model would assign these two vintages the same incentive based on their origination snapshots.

On the right, ELTV by vintage. The 2007 vintage shows ELTV rising initially — home prices fell, eroding equity — before recovering. The 2020 vintage shows steady ELTV decline as appreciation built equity rapidly. Again, completely invisible to a static model."

---

Slide 5 — Coefficient comparison
"This chart compares how the two macro features shared across both models — unemployment and HPI — behave in each context. The left panel shows all four time-varying features in TV Cox; the right panel shows only the two origination-era macro features in static Cox.

Three things stand out.

First, rate_incentive is the dominant positive predictor in TV Cox and isn't in the static model at all. The static model doesn't include rate_incentive because at origination it equals zero for almost every loan — everyone locks in at close to market rates. Only in the TV Cox model does this feature carry real information, because it updates monthly as market rates move.

Second, unemployment flips sign. In the static model, higher unemployment at origination correlates with slower prepayment — loans originated during downturns tend to be in less active refi markets. In the TV model, current monthly unemployment goes positive. The reason is multicollinearity: high-unemployment periods like 2009 to 2012 coincided with Fed easing and low mortgage rates. So monthly unemployment partially proxies for loose monetary conditions rather than borrower distress directly.

Third, ELTV is negative in TV Cox — as the current loan-to-value rises, refinancing is suppressed. This channel is completely invisible in the static model, where ELTV would just equal the origination LTV."

---

Slide 6 — Model performance
"The comparison table uses landmark C-index at fixed horizons — for each horizon T*, we snapshot each loan's covariate state at that time and score it, then measure concordance for who actually prepaid by T*. This is the right metric for time-varying models; the overall C-index conflates timing with probability.

The log-likelihood gain of +1,996 confirms the monthly updates carry substantially more information than the origination snapshot.

TV Cox beats static at T=12 through T=60. The gains are large at short and medium horizons: +0.072 at T=12, +0.060 at T=24, +0.045 at T=36, and +0.021 at T=60. The peak at T=24 is where most voluntary prepayments happen — the first active refinancing window where current rate incentive is the dominant driver.

But at T=84 and T=120, static Cox overtakes TV Cox by 0.018 and 0.037. The crossover is around T=70 months. The reason is selection: loans still alive at 7 or 10 years are a heavily filtered group — low-incentive, high-ELTV, locked-in borrowers who haven't found it worth refinancing. Their current covariate state at T=84 reflects why they haven't prepaid yet, not how risky they are going forward. The monthly-updated features backfire here. Static Cox avoids this trap by using the origination snapshot, which doesn't carry the near-exit contamination. The fix is a landmark super-model that conditions on surviving to a reference age before projecting forward."

---

Slide 7 — Key Takeaways
"To summarize: the +1,996 log-likelihood gain confirms monthly-updated covariates add substantial information. The short-horizon gains are large — +0.060 at T=24 with 100K loans — so the time-varying channel is real, not a sample-size artifact compared to Part B's 1.85M-loan macro Cox. Rate incentive is the key driver because at origination it's near zero for every loan; only monthly updates make it informative. The long-horizon picture is equally important: TV Cox falls below static at T=84 and T=120, with the crossover around 70 months. This is not a failure of the approach — it's a known structural limitation of the landmark C-index metric applied to TV models, and the fix is a landmark super-model. Static Cox's remarkable flatness across all horizons — barely moving from 0.609 to 0.620 across 10 years — tells you that origination features give steady, horizon-independent discrimination. TV Cox gives larger gains where it matters most — the first five years — at the cost of degrading beyond that."
