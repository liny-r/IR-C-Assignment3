Part E(iv) — Scenario Analysis Speaker Script
=============================================

Slide 1 — Title
"For the last part, we did stress-testing prepayment models under interest rate shocks and translating that into MBS cash flow projections."

---

Slide 2 — Setup: why TV Cox is the primary model
"The central question in scenario analysis is: if the Fed cuts rates by 300 basis points tomorrow, what happens to prepayment speed? The answer depends entirely on whether your model has a market-rate channel.

The TV Cox model from E(ii) does, because rate_incentive — the spread between the origination coupon and the current market rate — is a live time-varying covariate. A 100bp rate cut immediately shifts that spread by +1pp, which flows through the model analytically as exp(β_ri × 1.0). No approximation needed.

Partner's DeepCox has no such channel. Its rate variable is the origination coupon — a fixed number. The only way to shock it is to pretend the loan was originated at a different rate, which is economically backwards: a lower coupon means less incentive to refinance relative to that coupon, so the model predicts less prepayment when rates fall. That's the wrong direction."

---

Slide 3 — Shock sensitivity
"This plot makes the point precisely. The blue dots — TV Cox fitted Δlog-hazard — sit exactly on the analytic formula line across all seven scenarios. A 300bp rate cut raises the log-hazard by +0.123; a 300bp hike lowers it by −0.123. The two lines overlap because the TV Cox rate channel is linear in rate_incentive, so there's no approximation error. The model is fully transparent and auditable — any regulator can verify the sensitivity from a single coefficient."

---

Slide 4 — Survival curves
"Here are the survival curves under seven scenarios. The ordering is exactly as expected — lower rates mean faster prepayment, lower survival. The ±300bp range shifts 10-year survival by 6.6 percentage points: from 10.2% in the deep-cut scenario to 16.8% in the deep-hike scenario. Median prepayment month shifts by 10 months. The monotonic ordering is a necessary consistency check — it passes cleanly."

---

Slide 5 — CPR response curve and 2D contour
"The left panel is the CPR response curve: annual CPR as a function of rate incentive at loan age 36 months. CPR ranges from 12.6% at −300bp to 16.5% at +400bp. This is not a true S-curve — TV Cox has a log-linear hazard, so the CPR response is exponential and convex: monotone increasing with no saturation. A true S-curve with a flat region out-of-the-money and burnout at the top requires a nonlinear model. The compressed range reflects the Ridge-penalized β_ri of 0.041 on the 500K-row subsample.

The right panel shows joint sensitivity over rate incentive and FICO on top, LTV on the bottom. Unlike what you might expect from E(i), both surfaces show nearly horizontal bands in the FICO and LTV directions. That's because the fitted TV Cox coefficients for FICO and LTV are near zero and not statistically significant — p=0.99 for FICO, p=0.76 for LTV. The 500K-row subsample and Ridge penalty don't give enough resolution to identify those effects separately from the baseline hazard. Rate incentive drives essentially all the CPR variation in this specification. The FICO and LTV constraints are real economic mechanisms — identified clearly in E(i) — but they require a larger time-varying training sample to surface here."

---

Slide 6 — MBS cash flows
"Finally, the cash flow projection. We run 200 Monte Carlo paths with 25bp noise around each scenario and compute WAL and price for a representative $300K, 5.0% coupon loan at a baseline market rate of 5.3% — the median over the 1999–2025 sample.

The baseline price is $99.07 — approximately at par, which is consistent with the baseline loan being only 30bp out-of-the-money. The WAL range across ±300bp is 0.58 years, and the price range is $18.55.

The key structural result is negative convexity: the price gain from a 300bp cut is $9.25, and the price loss from a 300bp hike is $9.30 — the upside is slightly smaller than the downside. That's the prepayment ceiling at work: when rates fall, borrowers prepay and return principal at par, capping the price appreciation. Monte Carlo uncertainty is $0.00–0.06, negligible relative to the scenario spread."

---

Slide 7 — Key Takeaways
"To summarize: time-varying covariates are not optional for rate scenario analysis — static models produce the wrong sign. TV Cox is both transparent and analytically exact. The rate sensitivity in our model is compressed relative to real agency MBS, and the two fixes are straightforward: lower the Ridge penalty and expand the training sample. Negative convexity is correctly captured. And the 2D contour reveals a limitation: FICO and LTV are absorbed into the baseline hazard at this sample size, producing flat contours — the joint constraint structure from E(i) is economically real but requires more data to surface in the time-varying specification."
